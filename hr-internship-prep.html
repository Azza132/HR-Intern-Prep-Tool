Framework

The STAR Method

Behavioural interview questions ask you about past experiences. Structuring your answer with STAR makes your response clear, compelling, and easy to follow.

Why STAR works

Interviewers use behavioural questions because past behaviour predicts future performance. STAR gives you a logical structure that keeps your answer concise and evidence-based ‚Äî exactly what HR hiring managers want to see.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Briefly describe the context and background. Keep it short ‚Äî this is just the setup, not the main event.

T

Task

Explain your specific role or responsibility in that situation. What were you expected to do or solve?

A

Action

This is your moment to shine. Describe the specific steps you took. Use "I" not "we". Be concrete and detailed.

R

Result

What was the outcome? Quantify where possible. Mention any lessons learned ‚Äî it shows self-awareness, which HR values highly.

üìå Example STAR Answer

Question: "Tell me about a time you handled a conflict between colleagues."

S ‚Äî During my second year at university, I was part of a group project where two team members had a significant disagreement over the direction of our presentation.

T ‚Äî As the informal project lead, I felt responsible for keeping the team productive and ensuring we met our deadline.

A ‚Äî I arranged a brief one-to-one conversation with each person to understand their perspective without judgement. I then facilitated a short group discussion where I reframed the disagreement as two valid approaches and asked the team to evaluate both against our objectives.

R ‚Äî We reached a consensus within an hour, incorporated the stronger elements of both ideas, and submitted on time. Our group received an A, and one of the team members later told me they appreciated how I handled it.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common STAR Mistakes to Avoid

  • Spending too long on the Situation ‚Äî keep it to 1‚Äì2 sentences
  • Saying "we did" instead of "I did" ‚Äî the interviewer wants to know YOUR contribution
  • Forgetting the Result ‚Äî always land the plane with an outcome
  • Being vague ‚Äî use numbers, timelines, and specifics wherever possible
  • Using a negative outcome without showing reflection ‚Äî always add what you learned

    Tips & Tricks

    Practical advice from preparation through to post-interview follow-up.

    01

    Research the company's HR department specifically

    Look at their LinkedIn, check if they've been in the news, and find out what HR software they use. Mentioning specific tools (Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, etc.) shows genuine interest.

    02

    Prepare 5‚Äì7 STAR stories

    These stories should be adaptable to different questions. Good themes: conflict resolution, working under pressure, showing initiative, learning from failure, and supporting others.

    03

    Know current HR trends

    Brush up on topics like DE&I (Diversity, Equity & Inclusion), hybrid working policies, employee wellbeing, and talent retention. These are hot topics every HR team is navigating.

    04

    Prepare smart questions to ask them

    Have 3‚Äì4 thoughtful questions ready. Try: "What does success look like in this internship?" or "How does the HR team support line managers here?" Avoid asking about salary or holidays first.

    05

    Practice aloud, not just in your head

    Record yourself or ask a friend to mock-interview you. Hearing your own answers reveals filler words, pacing issues, and gaps you'd never notice just thinking through answers.

    06

    Plan your logistics

    Know exactly how you're getting there (or how your tech works for video). Being late or flustered before you even start costs you confidence points. Arrive or log in early.

    01

    Take a breath before answering

    It's completely fine to say "That's a great question, let me take a moment to think." A brief pause looks considered, not blank. Never rush into a rambling answer.

    02

    Mirror the interviewer's energy

    If they're warm and casual, relax a little. If they're formal, stay professional. Adaptability is a core HR skill ‚Äî demonstrating it in the room is powerful.

    03

    Use HR language naturally

    Slip in terms like "employee engagement," "retention strategies," "onboarding," or "talent pipeline" when relevant. It signals you understand the field, not just the job title.

    04

    Show genuine curiosity about people

    HR is fundamentally about people. Let your interest in human behaviour, fairness, and development come through. Interviewers are often assessing whether you'd be trusted by employees.

    05

    Be honest about what you don't know

    If asked about something you haven't encountered yet, say so ‚Äî then pivot to how you'd find out or learn. Honesty and learning agility are prized in HR.

    06

    Watch your body language

    Maintain natural eye contact, sit up straight, and nod when listening. In video interviews, look at your camera, not the screen. Turn off notifications and find good lighting.

    01

    Send a thank-you email within 24 hours

    Keep it brief, professional, and specific ‚Äî reference something from the conversation. This is often the deciding factor between two equally strong candidates.

    02

    Reflect on what went well and what didn't

    Write notes immediately after while it's fresh. What questions caught you off guard? What would you answer differently? Use this for your next interview.

    03

    Follow up politely if you haven't heard back

    If they gave you a timeline and it's passed, one polite follow-up email is perfectly appropriate. It shows you're genuinely interested, not just casting a wide net.

    04

    If rejected, ask for feedback

    Not all companies offer it, but asking professionally shows maturity. Frame it as: "I'd appreciate any feedback that could help me grow." Sometimes they'll share something invaluable.

    ‚úì Do

    • Tailor your answers to the specific company and role
    • Use specific examples with measurable outcomes
    • Show enthusiasm for people and HR as a career
    • Demonstrate self-awareness and a growth mindset
    • Research the company's culture and values beforehand
    • Ask thoughtful questions at the end
    • Speak confidently but not arrogantly
    • Follow up after the interview

    ‚úó Don't

    • Badmouth previous employers or teachers
    • Give generic, vague answers with no examples
    • Say "I'm a people person" without backing it up
    • Forget to mention YOUR specific contribution ("we" vs "I")
    • Lie or exaggerate ‚Äî HR professionals are trained to detect this
    • Ask only about pay, holidays, or perks first
    • Ramble ‚Äî keep answers to 2‚Äì3 minutes max
    • Forget to send a follow-up thank you
